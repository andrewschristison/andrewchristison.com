# Obviously Awesome

## Core Idea

Most products are underpositioning themselves — not because the product is weak, but because the team is too close to it to see it clearly. *Obviously Awesome* by April Dunford argues that positioning is not a marketing exercise. It is the foundational decision that determines who you sell to, how you describe what you do, and why customers choose you over alternatives.

Bad positioning makes a good product invisible. Great positioning makes the right customers say "this is exactly what I've been looking for."

Positioning is not your tagline. It is the context you establish in the buyer's mind before they evaluate you.

---

## The Five Components of Positioning

### 1. Competitive Alternatives — What would they do if you didn't exist?
The real competition is not other software products. It is whatever your target customer would do instead. For most dojo owners, the alternative to DojoFlow is not Spark Membership — it's a spreadsheet and a text thread.

Understanding the true alternative reveals the actual comparison the customer is making. They're not comparing DojoFlow to Kicksite feature-for-feature. They're comparing it to their current cobbled-together system of spreadsheets, Square, and Mailchimp.

**Why this matters:** If you position against Spark Membership, you implicitly tell customers they should be evaluating both. If you position against "spreadsheets and Square," you anchor the comparison where you win decisively.

### 2. Unique Attributes — What do you do that the alternatives can't?
List every capability, feature, or characteristic that is genuinely differentiated from the competitive alternatives you identified. Be honest — if a competitor also has it, it's not unique.

**DojoFlow unique attributes (vs. spreadsheets + Square):**
- Silent churn detection — surfaces students who are drifting before they quit
- Magic Text — AI-drafted messages written with the right tone for martial arts families
- Belt readiness badges — computed from promotion history, not manually tracked
- Walk-in trial capture with built-in follow-up flag
- Family billing unit with contact resolution rules

**DojoFlow unique attributes (vs. Spark Membership / Kicksite):**
- Modern UI built for how senseis actually work (mobile-first, class-view check-in)
- AI-drafted communications with human review (not just templates)
- Transparent pricing (if/when live) — no markup on processing fees

### 3. Value Themes — Why do those attributes matter to the customer?
Unique attributes are features. Value themes are the outcomes those features enable. The customer cares about the theme, not the attribute.

| Unique Attribute | Value Theme |
|-----------------|-------------|
| Silent churn detection | No student leaves without the sensei knowing |
| Magic Text | Communicate like a person, not a system |
| Belt readiness badges | Belt tests run without scrambling |
| Walk-in capture + follow-up flag | No trial student falls through the cracks |
| Family billing unit | One invoice per household — no confusion |

**The value theme for DojoFlow collapses to one sentence:** "Every student keeps progressing and no one leaves silently."

This is the positioning anchor. Every other message is a supporting argument.

### 4. Target Customer — Who is the best fit?
Not every dojo owner is the right customer. Positioning sharpens when you name exactly who it's for. A message that tries to reach everyone reaches no one.

**Best-fit customer profile for DojoFlow (pilot-validated):**
- Owner-operator or family-run dojo (not franchised or corporate chain)
- 30–200 active students
- Currently managing admin with a mix of spreadsheets, text messages, and manual follow-up
- Cares deeply about student outcomes and parent trust — not just revenue
- Frustrated by the gap between how much they care and how little visibility they have

**Anti-fit (do not target yet):**
- Large multi-location chains (needs enterprise features not yet built)
- Senseis who treat admin as a solved problem (no pull toward the product)
- Tournament-focused competitive programs (different jobs than a family rec program)

### 5. Market Category — What frame of reference do you set?
The market category you claim determines the expectations the customer arrives with. Choose wrong and you're evaluated against the wrong competitors or dismissed as too niche.

**Options for DojoFlow:**
- "Dojo management software" — accurate but puts you in a crowded category evaluated on feature parity
- "Student retention platform for martial arts schools" — positions around the job, not the category; differentiating but may require education
- "The anti-spreadsheet for dojo owners" — positions against the true alternative; works for early adopters who self-identify as outgrowing spreadsheets

**Recommended framing for the pilot phase:** "The dojo management tool built around one job: keeping every student progressing." This is category-creating without being alienating — buyers understand "dojo management" but are immediately differentiated by the job framing.

## The Positioning Exercise

Run this before writing any homepage copy, pitch deck, or sales email:

1. List the competitive alternatives (what does the buyer do today?)
2. List your unique attributes relative to those alternatives
3. Map each attribute to a value theme (the outcome the customer cares about)
4. Identify the customer segment that values those themes most urgently
5. Choose the market category that makes the value themes obvious

The output is a positioning statement:

> For [target customer], who [situation/pain],  
> DojoFlow is the [market category]  
> that [value theme].  
> Unlike [competitive alternative], DojoFlow [unique differentiator].

**DojoFlow version:**
> For owner-operated martial arts schools with 30–200 students, who are losing students silently because they have no way to catch drift before it becomes departure,  
> DojoFlow is the student retention platform  
> that flags at-risk students and drafts the right message before anyone leaves.  
> Unlike spreadsheets and Square, DojoFlow connects attendance, communication, and belt progression in one place — so the sensei always knows who needs attention.

## Common Failure Modes

- **Positioning by feature list** — describing what the product does instead of what it makes possible
- **Targeting everyone** — "any martial arts school" is not a target customer; it's an abdication of positioning
- **Wrong competitive frame** — comparing yourself to software competitors when the buyer's real alternative is a spreadsheet
- **Category confusion** — claiming to be "all-in-one" without defining all-in-one for what; the category must resonate with the buyer, not impress the team
- **Value themes too abstract** — "efficiency" and "saves time" are not value themes; "no student leaves without a follow-up" is
- **Repositioning too early** — changing positioning before validating it with the target customer; let the pilot signal whether the frame resonates before rewriting the homepage

## Applied to DojoFlow

Current positioning gap: the product has a clear core job ("reduce silent churn") but the market category is undefined and the competitive frame is implicit. Before expanding beyond the White Crane pilot:

1. **Validate the competitive alternative claim** — confirm that White Crane's actual workflow was spreadsheet + text, not a prior SaaS tool. The positioning depends on this.
2. **Test the value theme resonance** — does "no student leaves silently" resonate with other senseis the way it did with White Crane? Or is the language specific to this pilot?
3. **Decide on the market category** — "dojo management software" for discoverability vs. "student retention platform" for differentiation. This affects SEO, cold outreach framing, and word-of-mouth.
4. **Build the 1-sentence positioning anchor** before writing the homepage — every piece of copy should be a specific argument for the anchor, not a general claim about the product.
