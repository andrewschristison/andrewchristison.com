# Made to Stick

## Core Idea

Most ideas fail not because they're bad but because they're forgettable. *Made to Stick* by Chip and Dan Heath identifies why some ideas survive — passed along, remembered, acted on — while others evaporate the moment the conversation ends.

The answer is not repetition or budget. It's structure. Sticky ideas share six traits, captured in the acronym SUCCESs. Apply them to product copy, onboarding, sales conversations, and feature announcements to make DojoFlow's value impossible to forget.

---

## The SUCCESs Framework

### 1. Simple — Find the core
Strip the idea to its single most important truth. Not a summary — the *essential* thing. Everything else is subordinate.

The enemy of simple is the "curse of knowledge": once you know something, you can't remember what it was like not to know it. You explain too much, qualify too often, and bury the lead.

**Test:** If someone could only remember one thing about DojoFlow, what should it be?
> "Keeps every student progressing so none leave silently."

That's the core. Every headline, onboarding step, and feature description should orbit it.

**SaaS application:** Your homepage doesn't need to explain your architecture. It needs to answer one question: "Is this for me, and does it solve my problem?" Lead with the outcome, not the mechanism.

### 2. Unexpected — Break the pattern
Attention is earned by violating expectations. The brain ignores the predictable. To hold attention, create a gap — state something that surprises, then fill the gap with your idea.

The gap technique: "Here's what you think is true. Here's what's actually true. Now you need to know why."

**DojoFlow example:**
> Most dojo owners think they lose students because of money. They don't. Students leave silently, weeks before anyone notices — and no one sends a single message.

That surprises a sensei who has been attributing churn to pricing. Now they want to know more.

**What not to do:** generic "we're different" claims ("modern, easy-to-use software") are the opposite of unexpected. They're exactly what every competitor says.

### 3. Concrete — Make it tangible
Abstract ideas are forgotten. Concrete ideas stick. Use sensory language, specific numbers, and real examples — not categories and concepts.

Abstract: "DojoFlow improves student retention."
Concrete: "When a student misses two weeks in a row, you get a flag — name, photo, last class date — and a pre-written message ready to send. Takes 30 seconds."

**SaaS application:** Describe the experience, not the feature. Not "attendance analytics" — "see which students are drifting before they're already gone."

**Martial arts marketing application:** Parents don't buy "character development." They buy "my kid will learn to look adults in the eye and shake their hand." Same thing; one is concrete.

### 4. Credible — Make it believable
Ideas need a source of credibility or they're dismissed. Credibility can come from external (authorities, data) or internal (vivid details, the "try it yourself" test) sources.

**External credibility:** "White Crane Martial Arts, Washington State — the pilot dojo."
A named, real dojo is more credible than "dojos across the country."

**Internal credibility (Sinatra Test):** Find the one example so impressive it does the work. "If we can handle belt promotions for a 200-student dojo, we can handle yours."

**Vivid detail:** Specificity signals truth. "Students who miss 14 days" is more credible than "students who miss class." The precision implies the claim was measured, not guessed.

**The try-it-yourself test:** Can you let the prospect verify the claim themselves? A free trial, a live demo with their own data, a screenshot of an actual student's profile. Let them touch it.

### 5. Emotional — Make people care
People act on feelings, not facts. To move someone, connect the idea to something they already care about — their identity, their fears, their hopes for the people they love.

The key insight: don't appeal to the person's self-interest abstractly. Appeal to their identity and the identity of the people they're responsible for.

**For the sensei:** They didn't become a martial arts instructor for the admin. They became one to change lives. DojoFlow's emotional hook is: "You got into this to help kids grow. We make sure none of them fall through the cracks."

**For parents:** "Your kid's sensei will know if they're struggling — before they give up."

**SaaS application:** Feature announcements that lead with capability ("we added bulk email") are emotionally inert. Reframe: "Now you can reach every at-risk family before they decide to quit."

### 6. Stories — Make it narrative
Stories are a simulation of experience. A well-told story transfers the emotional reality of a situation directly into the listener's mind. They feel what the protagonist felt. They're already thinking about how they'd handle it.

**Story structure that works:**
- A challenge or problem the protagonist faces
- The moment they encounter the solution
- The concrete outcome — what changed

**DojoFlow story:**
> Grandmaster Nicholls had a student — good kid, showed up every week for two years. Then life got busy. Missed a class, then two, then a month. By the time she noticed, he'd already moved on. She never had a way to catch it early. That's what DojoFlow is for.

This story communicates the core job without a single feature bullet. The listener fills in the rest themselves.

## How to Apply It

**Score your copy against SUCCESs before shipping it.** For each piece of messaging — homepage, onboarding email, feature announcement — ask:
- Simple: Is there one clear takeaway?
- Unexpected: Does it violate a false assumption?
- Concrete: Is there a specific example or sensory detail?
- Credible: Is there a source, a number, or a verifiable claim?
- Emotional: Does it connect to what the reader actually cares about?
- Stories: Is there a protagonist facing a real situation?

You don't need all six. Two or three well-executed traits beat six poorly executed ones.

---

## Common Failure Modes

- **The curse of knowledge** — writing for people who already understand the problem, not people who haven't felt it yet
- **Feature announcements as messages** — "we added X" is not a message; "now you can do Y" is
- **Abstract benefits** — "powerful," "modern," "easy-to-use" are noise; specifics are signal
- **Generic stories** — a story about "a typical dojo owner" is not a story; a story about Grandmaster Nicholls is
- **Burying the lead** — the sticky version of your message is usually in the last paragraph you write; move it to the first

Core sticky message (already validated):
> "Help me keep every student progressing so none leave silently."

This message is Simple (one idea), Emotional (connects to why senseis became instructors), and Concrete (silent churn is a specific, recognizable fear). Build all copy around it.

Priority applications:

3. **Feature announcements** — reframe from capability to outcome before shipping
