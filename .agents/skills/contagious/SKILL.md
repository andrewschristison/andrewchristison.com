# Contagious

## Core Idea

Word of mouth drives more purchasing decisions than advertising, yet most businesses treat it as something that happens to them rather than something they design for. *Contagious* by Jonah Berger identifies six principles — STEPPS — that explain why some ideas, products, and stories spread while others don't.

For local businesses like martial arts schools, word of mouth is not a nice-to-have — it is the primary growth mechanism. A family who had a great experience and talks about it is worth more than any Google Ad. DojoFlow's growth depends on creating conditions where senseis and parents can't help but mention it.

---

## The STEPPS Framework

### S — Social Currency
People share things that make them look good. They talk about what makes them seem knowledgeable, connected, or ahead of the curve.

**The principle:** Build remarkable-ness into the product. If the thing itself is worth talking about, people will talk about it.

**For senseis:** A sensei who sends a personalized, well-crafted message to a parent who didn't expect it looks attentive and professional. That sensei will mention how they do it. "I have this tool that helps me draft the right message..." is word of mouth.

**For parents:** Belt promotions are inherently shareable — a parent who gets a "Kai just earned Blue Belt" message with a congratulations note shares it with family. Make promotion moments worthy of sharing.

**Design implication:** Every moment where the product makes the user look good is a word-of-mouth trigger. Identify these moments and make them more visible and more shareable.

### T — Triggers
People talk about what's top of mind. Ideas that are linked to everyday cues get mentioned more often than ideas that exist in isolation.

**The principle:** Connect your product to frequent environmental cues so it gets recalled and mentioned in the right moments.

**For DojoFlow:** The trigger is the dojo itself — when a parent drops their kid off, when a sensei reviews attendance before class, when a student gets a message between sessions. These are natural moments to reinforce the association.

**Trigger engineering:**
- Notification timing matters: an at-risk flag that surfaces Monday morning, before the week's classes start, is more likely to be acted on and discussed than one that surfaces on a Saturday evening
- Subject lines for emails should trigger recall of the specific student's situation, not just announce a category

### E — Emotion
When we care, we share. High-arousal emotions — excitement, awe, anxiety, awe — drive sharing more than low-arousal emotions like contentment.

**The principle:** Don't aim for satisfaction. Aim for the emotional moments that make people want to tell someone.

**High-arousal moments in a dojo:**
- A student earns their black belt after years of training — the family is emotional; the sensei is emotional; this is the moment
- A student who was struggling comes back after the sensei reached out — the sensei is relieved and proud
- A parent learns their kid hasn't missed a class in six months — they feel affirmed in the decision to enroll

**Design implication:** Surfaces and notifications around these high-arousal moments should feel commensurate with the moment. A milestone email for 100 classes attended should feel more significant than a routine absence reminder.

### P — Public
People imitate what they can observe. If a behavior is visible, it spreads. If it's invisible, it doesn't.

**The principle:** Make private behaviors public, and make your product's use visible to others who might adopt it.

**For DojoFlow:** The product is currently invisible — a sensei using it doesn't signal that fact to other senseis. Growth requires making the product's outputs visible.

**Visibility mechanisms:**
- Email footers: "Sent via DojoFlow" on magic-text messages to families (with opt-out)
- Belt promotion certificates or milestone summaries that families share
- Dojo-to-dojo referrals: senseis are often connected through tournament circuits and associations

**What not to do:** "Powered by DojoFlow" branding on parent-facing screens without explicit opt-in; the sensei's trust depends on the tool feeling professional, not promotional.

### P — Practical Value
People share useful things. News you can use travels faster than abstract ideas.

**The principle:** Make the value of your product concrete and immediately applicable — something people can act on or pass along.

**Practical value DojoFlow can generate:**
- An insight the sensei didn't have before: "3 students are at risk of lapsing this week — here's who"
- A template that a sensei can pass along to a colleague: "Here's the message I send when a student misses two weeks"
- An outcome the parent can point to: "The dojo actually told me when my kid's attendance dropped"

**Shareable practical value:** Create reports, summaries, or insights that senseis naturally forward to each other or share in martial arts instructor communities. A retention benchmark ("schools using proactive outreach retain X% more students") is the kind of stat that gets forwarded.

### S — Stories
Information travels inside stories. A narrative wrapping — protagonist, conflict, resolution — makes the idea sticky and shareable in a way that facts alone are not.

**The principle:** Give your customers a story to tell about you, not just a feature to describe.

**The story DojoFlow needs to enable:**
> "We had a student who started drifting — missing classes, going quiet. Before I would have found out when they stopped showing up entirely. Now I get a flag, I send a message, and more often than not they come back. One of my students told her parents the sensei personally reached out when she missed class. The parents renewed their membership the same week."

This story spreads through parent WhatsApp groups, dojo owner forums, and martial arts associations. It doesn't describe the software — it describes the outcome.

**Design implication:** When a proactive outreach leads to a student returning, surface that outcome in the product. "Following your message, Kai came back to class." That creates the closing beat of the story the sensei tells.

## How to Apply STEPPS

Before launching any feature or marketing initiative, run it through the framework:
- Does using it give the sensei or parent **social currency**?
- Is it linked to a frequent **trigger** in their daily routine?
- Does it connect to a high-arousal **emotion**?
- Is the behavior **public** in any way, or can we make it so?
- Does it offer **practical value** that is worth passing along?
- Does it enable or emerge from a compelling **story**?

You don't need all six. Two or three activated principles create meaningful word-of-mouth potential.

---

## Common Failure Modes

- **Designing for satisfaction instead of arousal** — happy customers don't share; amazed, moved, or relieved customers do
- **Assuming quality is enough** — great products don't automatically spread; they spread when the conditions for sharing exist
- **Ignoring triggers** — building a product people love but don't think about at the moment they're talking to other senseis
- **No story hook** — producing good outcomes but giving users no narrative frame to share them in
- **Forced virality** — mandatory referral prompts that feel like a toll; social currency has to be genuine, not coerced

---

## Applied to DojoFlow

DojoFlow's word-of-mouth surface area is currently small — the product is invisible to everyone except the sensei using it. Growth requires expanding that surface area deliberately.

Priority STEPPS opportunities:
1. **Social Currency:** Magic Text messages that are visibly thoughtful — when a parent receives a well-crafted personal note, they mention it
2. **Emotion:** Belt milestone moments — design these to be share-worthy for families (certificate PDFs, milestone summaries)
3. **Stories:** Build the feedback loop that closes the "I reached out → student came back" story; surface retention outcomes in the dashboard
4. **Public:** Optional "Sent via DojoFlow" footer on family-facing messages; instructor community presence (Facebook groups, USAT forums)
5. **Practical Value:** Retention benchmarks and insights that senseis can reference in conversations with peers
