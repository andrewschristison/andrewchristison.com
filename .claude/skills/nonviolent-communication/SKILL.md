# Nonviolent Communication

## Core Idea

*Nonviolent Communication* (NVC) by Marshall Rosenberg is a framework for speaking and listening that creates genuine connection and reduces conflict. Its premise: most human conflict — in relationships, workplaces, and organisations — stems from patterns of communication that block empathy: evaluation masquerading as observation, judgement framing feelings, and demands that deny the other person's autonomy.

NVC is not about being soft or avoiding difficult conversations. It is about being precise: saying exactly what you observe, what you feel, what you need, and what you're asking for — in a way the other person can actually hear.

---

## The Four Components

Every NVC expression moves through four steps. Each step has a discipline that makes it work.

### 1. Observation (not evaluation)

State what you observe — the specific actions or events you can point to — without mixing in judgement, interpretation, or evaluation.

**Evaluation (blocks connection):**
> "You never follow through on what you say."

**Observation (opens connection):**
> "The last three times we agreed on a ship date, the feature wasn't ready on that date."

The distinction is not about being kind — it is about being accurate. An evaluation describes your conclusion. An observation describes the data. The other person can dispute your conclusion; they cannot reasonably dispute what happened.

**The test:** can you film it? If you can point a camera at it and the footage shows it, it is an observation. If the footage would show something but reasonable people would describe it differently, it is evaluation.

### 2. Feeling (not thinking)

Name the emotion you are experiencing — not your interpretation of someone else's behaviour, not a disguised accusation, not a cognitive assessment.

**Thinking disguised as feeling (doesn't build connection):**
> "I feel that you don't care about quality."
> "I feel manipulated."
> "I feel like this is going in circles."

**Genuine feeling (connects):**
> "I feel frustrated."
> "I feel anxious."
> "I feel disappointed."

The difference: "I feel that..." is always followed by a thought. "I feel [emotion word]" is a feeling. Disguised thoughts-as-feelings assign responsibility to the other person; feelings locate the experience in yourself.

**Common feelings when needs are met:** grateful, relieved, joyful, engaged, hopeful, confident, moved, satisfied

**Common feelings when needs are not met:** anxious, frustrated, disappointed, exhausted, confused, sad, overwhelmed, resentful

### 3. Need (not strategy)

Identify the underlying need — the universal human value that is currently satisfied or unsatisfied. Needs are not requests, not strategies, not specific people. They are fundamental.

**Strategy (specific, creates conflict if denied):**
> "I need you to update the task list every day."

**Need (universal, creates understanding):**
> "I need transparency about what's in progress so I can plan."

Rosenberg's key insight: all conflict is a conflict between strategies, not between needs. Two people's needs are almost never incompatible. Their strategies may be. When you locate the need, you open the possibility of finding a different strategy that meets both people's needs.

**Universal human needs (partial list):**
- Connection: belonging, closeness, understanding, being heard, trust
- Autonomy: choice, freedom, independence, creativity
- Integrity: authenticity, honesty, meaning
- Safety: security, predictability, consistency
- Contribution: to matter, to be useful, to create
- Clarity: understanding, information, transparency

### 4. Request (not demand)

Make a specific, positive, present-tense request — what you're asking for now, stated as an action, not an abstraction. A request becomes a demand if the other person believes they will be blamed, punished, or manipulated if they say no.

**Demand (closes the conversation):**
> "You need to communicate better."

**Vague request (creates ambiguity):**
> "Could you keep me more in the loop?"

**Specific, present-tense request (actionable):**
> "Would you be willing to send a one-line status note by end of day when a task is blocked?"

**The request test:** if the other person says no, can you stay curious about their reason — or do you feel provoked, hurt, or triggered? If the latter, it was a demand.

## Empathic Listening

NVC is not only a speaking framework. Empathic listening applies the same four components to what you're hearing — receiving the other person's message as observation, feeling, need, and request rather than as accusation, complaint, or criticism.

**What someone says:**
> "This feature has been 'almost done' for three weeks."

**What they might be expressing:**
- Observation: feature has been in-progress for three weeks
- Feeling: frustrated, anxious
- Need: reliability, predictability, honesty about scope
- Request: (unstated) a realistic estimate or a scope cut

Empathic listening doesn't mean agreeing. It means receiving the message at the level of need rather than reacting to the surface words. The response from empathy is: "It sounds like you're frustrated because you need to be able to plan, and three weeks of uncertainty has made that hard — is that right?"

This is not a therapy technique. It is precision: instead of defending against the accusation implicit in the words, you respond to what the person actually needs.

## Jackal and Giraffe Language

Rosenberg uses two animal metaphors:

**Jackal language:** evaluates, judges, demands, blames, moralises. "You always...", "You never...", "That's wrong", "You should...". Jackals operate from a worldview of right/wrong, good/bad. Jackal language may be internally motivated (shame, guilt, fear of punishment) or externally directed (demanding compliance, assigning blame).

**Giraffe language:** observes, feels, needs, requests. Giraffes are Rosenberg's symbol for NVC — tall enough to see others' perspective, heart close to the ground. Giraffe language does not judge; it inquires.

The point is not to eliminate jackal thoughts — everyone has them. The point is to translate: when you notice a jackal thought, ask what need is behind it, and express that need instead of the evaluation.

## Expressing Anger

Rosenberg's treatment of anger is counterintuitive: anger is not caused by other people. It is caused by judgements we make in response to what others do. The other person's behaviour is the stimulus; our evaluation of it is the cause.

**Jackal expression of anger:**
> "You made me furious by missing that deadline."

**NVC expression of anger:**
> "When I saw that the feature wasn't ready on Thursday (observation), I felt furious (feeling), because I need reliability in our commitments (need). Would you be willing to tell me if a deadline is at risk before it passes? (request)"

The NVC form takes longer. It also gets heard — because it doesn't trigger the other person's defence.

The practical discipline: before expressing anger, pause. Identify the judgement. Find the need underneath it. Then speak from the need.

## Self-Empathy

NVC applies inward as well. Rosenberg argues that harsh self-judgement — guilt, shame, self-blame — is a form of violence directed at oneself. It is also ineffective: people driven by guilt perform obligations instead of creating from care.

The alternative is self-empathy: observing your own actions, naming the feeling, identifying the need that wasn't met, and making a request of yourself going forward — without punishment, shame, or moralising.

**Self-judgement:**
> "I wasted the whole day and got nothing done. I'm useless."

**Self-empathy:**
> "I didn't complete anything today (observation). I feel deflated (feeling). I need a sense of momentum and progress (need). What's one thing I can finish in the next hour? (request of self)"

## NVC in Feedback and Code Review

Software development is full of situations where NVC is relevant: code review, postmortems, scope disagreements, delayed features. Most feedback in these contexts is given in jackal form.

**Jackal code review:**
> "This is over-engineered. You always abstract things before they need to be."

**NVC code review:**
> "This abstraction layer adds three files and an interface for what is currently one use case (observation). I'm concerned (feeling) that this will slow down iteration before we've validated the approach (need: simplicity at this stage). Would you be willing to inline it for now and extract later if a second use case appears? (request)"

The NVC version is longer. It is also more likely to result in agreement — because it states the reasoning (need) rather than making a character assessment.

## Common Failure Modes

- **"I feel that..." constructions** — these are thoughts, not feelings; they assign responsibility rather than locating experience; they trigger defence rather than empathy
- **Needs stated as strategies** — "I need you to check in daily" is a strategy; if the other person says no, you're stuck; name the need (transparency, reliability) to open negotiation
- **Requests as demands** — if the implicit message is "say no and something bad happens," it's a demand; demands produce compliance or resistance, not connection
- **Empathic listening as capitulation** — reflecting someone's feeling is not agreeing with them; it is receiving their message; understanding does not require conceding
- **Observation contaminated with evaluation** — "you frequently miss deadlines" mixes observation with evaluation; "the last three deadlines were missed" is pure observation; the latter is harder to dispute and easier to hear
- **Skipping self-empathy** — expressing NVC while internally flooded with unprocessed anger or judgement; the words are right but the energy is wrong; the other person feels the hostility beneath the structure

## Applied to DojoFlow

**User feedback situations:**
When a pilot user (Grandmaster, Master Nicholls) expresses frustration with the platform, apply empathic listening before defending or explaining:

- **What they say:** "The system is confusing — I couldn't figure out how to add a new student."
- **Hear it as:** frustrated + need for clarity and control + request for a simpler path to the action they use most
- **Response:** "It sounds like the navigation isn't obvious — is that right? What were you trying to do when you got stuck?"

**Scope conversations:**
When disagreeing about what to build, use NVC structure to locate the real disagreement:

- **Observation:** "The billing feature has been in the roadmap for three months and isn't started."
- **Feeling:** "I feel anxious about deferring it further."
- **Need:** "I need confidence that revenue collection will be possible before we sign more pilots."
- **Request:** "Would you be willing to look at a minimal billing flow — manual invoicing only — that we could build in one week?"

**Magic Text message drafts:**
The messages DojoFlow helps senseis draft are, structurally, NVC communications. The most effective follow-up messages:
- Open with a factual observation, not an accusation: "We haven't seen you in a few weeks" not "You've been missing class"
- Name concern without pressure: "We wanted to check in" not "We're worried you might quit"
- End with a specific, non-demanding request: "Let us know if there's anything getting in the way — we'd love to see you on the mat" not "Please come back"

This is why Magic Text drafts need human review: the AI may default to jackal-adjacent language that makes senseis sound demanding or presumptuous. The review step is where the message gets its human warmth.
