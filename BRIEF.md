# andrewchristison.com — Creative Brief v1

## What This Is

A personal site for Andrew Christison that functions as an environment, not a document. It should feel like arriving at someone's desk, workshop, or kitchen table — a place where work and life aren't separated into tabs but coexist naturally because they belong to the same person.

The site earns the reaction **"oooo, cool. what's this?"** within three seconds of landing, and rewards anyone who stays to look around.

## What This Is Not

- Not a portfolio site with personal flavor bolted on
- Not a landing page optimized for conversion
- Not a Squarespace template
- Not trying to look like every other founder's site with a hero section, three value props, and a CTA
- Has no audience it's trying to impress — it has visitors it's trying to intrigue

## Core Concept: The Desktop

The site is a Windows 95-inspired desktop environment. Not a pixel-perfect emulator — the structural language of that era (visible window chrome, title bars, defined borders, a taskbar, depth through shadow and inset) provides a system that's immediately recognizable and navigable without instruction. The aesthetic is felt more than replicated: someone took the bones of a 1995 operating system and lived in it for thirty years, and now it's full of their life.

### Why This Metaphor Works

A traditional website says "I've organized information for you to consume." A desktop says "I've left my world open for you to explore." That reframes the visitor from audience to guest. They're not being pitched — they're being welcomed into a space that already exists whether they showed up or not.

This solves the core tension: how do you present martial arts next to lifecycle marketing next to homesteading without it feeling like a bizarre LinkedIn profile? You don't present them. You let someone discover them in the same space, the way you'd discover someone's interests by looking around their actual room. Context does the work that explanation can't.

The desktop metaphor also scales gracefully — six icons and two open windows doesn't look empty (it looks like a workspace), and fifteen open windows doesn't look cluttered (it looks like someone's busy).

## Target Feeling (First 3 Seconds)

"oooo, cool. what's this?"

Curiosity before comprehension. The visitor leans in before they know what they're looking at.

## Design Direction

### Palette
Warmer than Microsoft grey. Aged paper, worn wood, something that evokes the Olympic Peninsula more than Redmond. Accent colors drawn from content — the portfolio window might pull Retencity's colors, the homestead pulls greens and earth tones, the writing space gets to be its own thing.

### Typography
One workhorse monospace for system UI (window titles, taskbar, labels). One font with more character for content inside the windows. Not pixel fonts unless the vibe specifically calls for it — pixel fonts risk pushing from "inspired by" to "cosplay."

### Chrome & Interaction
Win95 bevels (light top-left, dark bottom-right shadow) adapted to the warmer palette. Windows have proper title bars with minimize, maximize, and close buttons. The feel should be tactile and satisfying.

### Sound
Subtle audio feedback — a soft chime on window open, click sounds, maybe a startup sound. Tasteful, not annoying. Easy to mute.

## The Writing Space

Called nothing definitive. "Notes" or "Desk" or "Shelf" or even just "—." The metaphor inside is a surface with objects on it — not a feed, not a blog roll, not a chronological list.

Each piece is an object: a book cover with a reaction, a sketch, a few sentences, a longer essay, a photo with a caption. Arranged spatially, not temporally. No "latest post" energy. No publish date unless it matters.

When the shelf has two things, it looks curated. When it has twenty, it looks like a collection. Neither state feels wrong.

The pieces can be anything: a reaction to C.G. Jung's biography, a half-formed thought about practice, a photo from a trail, a quote that won't leave his head. No format or cadence commitment. The only commitment is that anything placed here was placed deliberately.

If this section gets ripped out, the desktop loses one icon — no structural damage.

## Window Behavior

Windows are fully interactive:
- **Draggable** by title bar
- **Minimizable** to taskbar (with indicator)
- **Closable** (with recovery — can reopen from desktop icon or start menu)
- **Z-index managed** — clicking a window brings it to front
- **Resizable** if feasible without excessive complexity

The taskbar is fixed at the bottom with:
- Start button/menu
- Open/minimized window indicators
- Clock (real-time)

Start menu contains all windows/sections plus links (LinkedIn, etc.) and fun items.

## Future Expansion

The desktop metaphor makes expansion trivial:
- **In Process podcast** → new icon on desktop when ready (subdomain or /inprocess)
- **DojoFlow / Banyan Stand** → new card in work window or its own icon
- **New writing pieces** → new objects on the shelf
- Nothing requires restructuring. The architecture is additive.

## Mobile Approach

Desktop metaphor doesn't translate directly to a phone screen. Mobile layout should preserve the spirit — perhaps a vertical stack of "windows" that feel like cards — without simulating a desktop on 375px. The experience can be different; it doesn't need to be the same. Desktop should feel like desktop.

## Technical Approach

- Single HTML file (or small bundle) with CSS and vanilla JS
- No framework, no build step
- Deployable anywhere (Netlify, Vercel, static host)
- Google Fonts loaded via CDN
- Sound via Web Audio API (no external audio files)
- Photos referenced from `/assets/photos/`

## Out of Scope for v1

- In Process podcast section
- DojoFlow / Banyan Stand
- CMS or writing tool — writing shelf is hand-coded for now
- Blog functionality, RSS, email capture
- SEO optimization beyond basic meta tags
- Dark mode (though could be a beautiful "display settings" Easter egg later)
