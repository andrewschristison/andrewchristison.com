# The Mom Test

## Core Idea

Most customer interviews are useless because people lie — not out of malice, but out of politeness. Your mom will tell you your idea is great. Investors tell you it's interesting. Potential customers say they'd definitely use it. None of that is signal.

Rob Fitzpatrick's rule: **Talk about their life, not your idea.** The goal is to learn facts about their behavior and problems, not to collect opinions about your solution.

A good conversation passes the Mom Test: even your mom couldn't give you a false positive if she tried, because you never asked her to judge your idea.

---

## The Three Rules

### 1. Talk about their life, not your idea

Bad: "Would you use an app that automatically flagged students who haven't attended in two weeks?"
Good: "Tell me about the last time a student just stopped coming. What did you do?"

The first question invites them to imagine using a hypothetical product (people are bad at this). The second question surfaces real behavior from real experience.

### 2. Ask about specifics in the past, not generalities or opinions about the future

Bad: "Do you often struggle to keep track of absent students?"
Good: "When did you last notice a student was drifting? What happened?"

People's past behavior is reliable. Their opinions about their future behavior are not.

### 3. Listen more than you talk

If you're talking more than 30% of the time, you're pitching, not learning. The moment you start explaining your solution, you've stopped doing discovery.

---

## Good vs. Bad Questions

| Bad | Why it fails | Good alternative |
|-----|-------------|-----------------|
| "Do you think attendance tracking is important?" | Prompts a yes; no signal | "How do you currently track who's coming?" |
| "Would you pay for this?" | Hypothetical; unreliable | "How much do you spend on [current solution] now?" |
| "Don't you hate doing this manually?" | Leading | "Walk me through how you handle this today." |
| "What features would you want?" | They design from imagination | "What's the most painful part of running your school?" |
| "Would this solve your problem?" | Seeking validation | "How do you solve this today? What's missing?" |

---

## The Three Types of Signal

**Compliments** — worthless for learning. "That's a great idea!" tells you nothing. Acknowledge and redirect: "I'm glad you like the direction. What does your current situation look like?"

**Fluff** — generic statements with no substance. "I'd definitely want something like that." "That sounds really useful." No commitment, no behavior, no specifics. Dig: "When did you last need something like that?"

**Facts, commitments, and actions** — actual signal:
- "We lose about 3 students a month and never know why."
- "I spend an hour every Sunday going through our attendance spreadsheet."
- "I tried [competitor] and stopped because..."
- A commitment to try a prototype
- Pre-payment for something that doesn't exist yet

---

## Digging for the Real Problem

People often describe symptoms, not root causes. They'll say "I need better reports" when the real problem is "I don't know who to call when I'm behind on revenue." Always ask one level deeper:

- "Why is that hard?"
- "What happens when you don't do that?"
- "How do you handle it today?"
- "What does that cost you?" (time, money, emotional energy)

The third "why" usually gets to something real.

---

## The Importance of Context

Always ask about context before solutions:
- When does this problem happen? (trigger)
- Who else is involved?
- What did you try before?
- What's the consequence of not solving it?

Context is what separates a real job-to-be-done from a surface-level complaint. "I need better attendance tracking" is a complaint. "Every time a student misses three classes and we don't reach out, they're usually gone for good — and I don't always notice until the next payment fails" is a job.

---

## Running a Good Interview

**Before:** Write down the three most important things you want to learn. Not questions — things you want to know. Let the conversation find its own path.

**Opening:** "Thanks for making time. I want to understand how you currently handle [domain], not pitch anything. Mind if I ask about your experience?" — signals you're learning, not selling.

**Middle:** Follow threads. When something sounds interesting, dig. "You mentioned X — can you say more about that?"

**Closing:** "Is there anything I didn't ask that you think I should know?" Often produces the most valuable signal.

**After:** Write up learnings immediately. Note exact quotes. Flag anything that surprised you. Update your opportunity tree.

---

## Common Failure Modes

- **The pitch-disguised-as-interview** — you explain the idea, they react to your explanation, not their real problem
- **Seeking validation** — you feel good after every conversation because people are nice; you haven't learned anything
- **Skipping the past** — only asking about hypothetical futures; no actionable signal
- **Premature solution conversation** — jumping to "so would you use X" before you understand their situation
- **Asking about willingness to pay too early** — it's a meaningful question only after you understand the job and the stakes

## Applied to DojoFlow

Questions for the White Crane pilot and future prospects:

**Churn:**
- "Tell me about the last student who left. Did you see it coming?"
- "When you notice a student has been absent a few weeks, what do you typically do?"

**Communication:**
- "How do you reach out to families right now? Walk me through a recent example."
- "When did you last send an email to your whole student list? What prompted it?"

**Operations:**
- "What does a typical Monday morning look like for you in terms of school admin?"
- "What's the most time-consuming non-teaching part of running your school?"

**Competition/alternatives:**
- "What tools are you using right now? What made you start using them?"
- "What's the thing that makes you want to throw them out the window?"
