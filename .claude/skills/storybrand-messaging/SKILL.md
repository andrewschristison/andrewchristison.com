# StoryBrand Messaging

## Core Idea

Customers don't buy products — they buy solutions to their problems. Your marketing should make the customer the hero of the story, not your product. The product is the guide that helps them succeed. If your landing page talks mostly about your company, you've made yourself the hero. The customer stopped reading.

Donald Miller's framework: every brand message should follow the 7-part story structure that every great narrative uses.

---

## The 7-Part Framework

### 1. A Character (the customer) who wants something
Lead with the customer's desired outcome, not your product's features.

**Wrong:** "DojoFlow is a martial arts management platform with attendance tracking, belt management, and automated messaging."

**Right:** "Keep every student progressing. Know who's at risk before they disappear."

The character wants a specific transformation. Name it. For DojoFlow's target sensei: they want to run a thriving school without it feeling like they're managing a spreadsheet business.

### 2. Has a Problem
Three levels:
- **External problem** — the tangible, practical issue (students go quiet and leave without warning)
- **Internal problem** — how the problem makes them feel (anxiety; am I doing enough? am I a good teacher?)
- **Philosophical problem** — the injustice or wrongness of the situation ("hard-working instructors shouldn't have to become software administrators to keep their students")

Most products only address the external problem. The best products (and best messaging) address all three. The internal problem is often what closes the sale.

### 3. And Meets a Guide
You are not the hero. You are Yoda to their Luke. Establish credibility without bragging:
- "Built by people who understand what it takes to run a school"
- Show that you understand their problem (empathy)
- Demonstrate that you have a solution (authority)

One sentence of empathy, one of authority. Then move on.

### 4. Who Gives Them a Plan
Remove the anxiety about "how does this work?" by giving a simple, clear plan:

3-step plan (maximum):
1. Enter your students
2. Set up your class schedule
3. Start seeing who needs attention — and reach out in seconds

Plans reduce anxiety. Complexity in the plan = lost customer.

### 5. And Calls Them to Action
Two types of CTA:
- **Direct CTA** — "Start Free Trial", "Get Started" — primary, transactional
- **Transitional CTA** — "See How It Works", "Watch a Demo" — for people not ready to commit

Most products have only a direct CTA and lose the people who need more convincing first.

### 6. That Helps Them Avoid Failure
Name the cost of not acting. The stakes need to be real:
- Students who miss two weeks and never hear from you are gone in a month
- Spreadsheets don't scale — you lose track of who's eligible to test, who's at risk, who has an expired payment
- Every student you lose silently is a failure to fulfill the school's purpose

Don't be morbid, but don't be timid. People are more motivated by loss than gain (loss aversion).

### 7. And Ends in Success
Paint the after picture:
- You know every student by name and situation, not just the ones who show up
- You send a check-in email in 30 seconds when someone goes quiet
- Belt tests happen on time because you know who's ready
- The school runs like a professional operation, not a side hustle

The success state should directly mirror the failure state. Each pain point you named should have a corresponding success image.

## The One-Liner

A one-liner is one sentence that makes the customer instantly understand the value. Structure:
`[Problem] + [Product] + [Result]`

For DojoFlow:
> "Most martial arts schools lose students silently — DojoFlow helps instructors catch at-risk students early and reach out before it's too late."

Or more aspirational:
> "DojoFlow gives martial arts instructors the tools to keep every student progressing — not just the ones who show up."

Test it on someone unfamiliar with the product. If they can't explain it back to you, simplify.

---

## Common Failure Modes

- **Ego copy** — "We are passionate about martial arts and dedicated to helping schools succeed" — nobody cares; talk about them
- **Feature lists as benefits** — "attendance tracking, belt management, Stripe payments" — these are features; the benefit is "you'll always know who's at risk and what to do about it"
- **The confusion tax** — unclear messaging makes customers work to understand your product; they don't; they leave
- **No failure state** — if you don't name what happens if they don't act, there's no urgency
- **Weak CTA** — "Learn More" is the weakest possible CTA; it's a delay mechanism, not a call to action

---

## Applied to DojoFlow

Current positioning: product-centric (feature descriptions). Opportunity to shift to:

**Tagline:** "Keep every student progressing."

**Sub-headline:** "DojoFlow helps martial arts instructors catch at-risk students early, reach out in seconds, and run their school like a professional — without touching a spreadsheet."

**Stakes:** "Students who go quiet for two weeks are usually gone within a month. Most schools never know why."

**Success image:** "See every student's status at a glance. Know who hasn't attended in two weeks. Send a personal follow-up in 30 seconds. Never lose a student to silence again."

This framing should inform every piece of UI copy — button labels, empty states, onboarding, email subject lines — not just the marketing site.
